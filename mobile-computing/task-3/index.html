B5G/6G is the Next Generation of Mobile Communications

B5G/6G is the next generation of mobile communications technology that offers stronger performance and meets future user needs effectively. 5G and LTE Advanced play an important role in the development of 6G.

In the future, 6G is expected to be able to answer the increasingly complex demands of cellular communication technology in everyday life.

5G Background

5G: A New Mobile Broadband Network

5G is becoming a new mobile broadband network, which will provide large-scale support services for the Internet of Things (IoT).

3GPP has completed 5G NR Release 16, which is the second 5G standard in 2020. It maximizes the range of 5G and achieves the expansion of potential new services, new radio spectrum and new possibilities of deployment.

The Release 16 comprehensively intensifies and extends the basis of Release 15 and accomplishes a vertical ecosystem with more mobility.

5G Smart Pole Technology

What is a 5G Smart Pole?

A 5G-based smart pole is an IoT-based streetlight pole that consists of streetlights as its hardware, IoT devices with different functions, and an intelligent network interface with 5G communications protocol.

A 5G-based smart pole is usually stationed at the LAN environment and connects to WAN via 5G in the street to perform these applications. The 5G-based pole also collects IoT data dynamically and provides various services.

The collected data are used to consider the suitability of the environment such as plant planting. A warning sign would be issued when the data exceeds the standard.

Experiment with DHT11

The research team used the DHT11 sensor combined with the 5G smart pole to detect temperature and humidity in the air for various applications.

Sensor Flow Process

The team designed a Sensor flow chart and combined it with Petri Net to verify the model works well from input to output:

  1. Start at sensor activation
  2. Check if environmental data is detected
  3. If not detected, make a notification
  4. If detected, collect data
  5. Check if value exceeds threshold
  6. If not exceeded, show normal status (LED light green)
  7. If exceeded, trigger alarm (LED light red)

Quality of Service (QoS) for B5G/6G Networking

6G networks are designed to deliver highly flexible quality of service (QoS). This allows for support of use cases with different requirements, including:

  • High data rates
  • Low latency
  • High reliability

The Difference between 6G and 5G:

6G requires more comprehensive QoS control than 5G. This is because 6G must support a wider range of use cases with more diverse requirements.

6G considers additional QoS dimensions such as security and sustainability.

Media Access Control (MAC) Scheduler

PHY and MAC Layers

The gNB (5G base station) MAC layer can ensure fair resource sharing between UEs (user equipment) to prevent interference. This allows the UE to achieve the desired throughput and latency for different applications, such as video streaming or online gaming.

6G, 5G, and LTE-advanced Wireless Networks use advanced technologies to achieve high performance and reliability. Efficient wireless resource allocation and adaptive MAC scheduling are critical to meeting diverse QoS requirements and achieving the full potential of these networks.

Q&A Session (Part 1 of 3)

Question from Gede Bramanda:

Good afternoon, Sir. My name is Bramanda. I would like to ask a question regarding the necessity of 5G in IoT implementation. Considering that most IoT devices transmit only small amounts of data, wouldn't 4G be sufficient in most cases? Or are there specific scenarios such as complex processing on the device that truly require 5G?

Answer: That's an excellent question. You're right that many IoT devices transmit relatively small amounts of data. For these devices, we've developed Narrowband IoT (NBIoT), which is actually designed to work within 4G/LTE networks and doesn't necessarily require full 5G implementation. NBIoT is optimized for low power consumption, low cost, and providing connectivity in challenging locations.

However, there are several scenarios where 5G becomes necessary for IoT:
1. For massive IoT deployments where thousands of devices need to connect simultaneously within a small area
2. For real-time applications requiring ultra-low latency, such as autonomous vehicles or industrial automation
3. For high-bandwidth IoT applications like surveillance systems or augmented reality

The advantage of 5G for IoT isn't just about speed, but also about network slicing capabilities, which allow dedicated virtual networks with specific quality of service guarantees for different applications.

Answer: Thank you for these comprehensive questions. Let me address the first two points:

1. Integration of 6G, AI, and IoT:
B5G/6G networks fundamentally transform how these technologies interact by supporting massive device connectivity at unprecedented scale. Where 5G can handle about 1 million devices per square kilometer, 6G aims to support 10 million or more. This enables truly ubiquitous sensing and data collection.

AI serves two critical roles in this integration: AI4Net (AI for Network) optimizes the network itself—managing resources, predicting congestion, and self-healing; while Net4AI (Network for AI) creates a communication infrastructure specifically designed to support distributed AI processing across edge devices, which leads us to TinyML.

2. Real-World Applications:
The applications are remarkably diverse, from smart cities to precision agriculture. For instance, our smart poles demonstrate how this fusion enables environmental monitoring, adaptive lighting, and security applications in urban spaces.

6G enhances IoT efficiency through Quality of AI Service (QoAIS) and AI as a Service (AIaaS) frameworks that ensure computational resources are allocated optimally. The ultra-low latency (potentially sub-millisecond) enables real-time applications like augmented reality or autonomous systems.

AI's role in analyzing IoT data is transformative. Through approaches like AI for Edge Services (AI4ES) and TinyML, we can perform complex analytics directly on the device without cloud dependency. This enables instantaneous decisions with privacy preservation. For example, a surveillance camera with TinyML can identify specific events of interest without sending constant video streams to the cloud.

Q&A Session (Part 3 of 3)

Questions from PUTU INDRA MAHARDIKA (Continued):

3. Implementation Challenges:
- What challenges may be faced in implementing this technology?
- What obstacles should be considered, particularly regarding data security and interoperability between devices?

Answer (Continued):
3. Implementation Challenges:
The primary challenges include energy efficiency for always-on devices, security at massive scale, and interoperability between diverse ecosystems. MAC scheduling becomes particularly complex when billions of devices need coordinated access to the network.

Security concerns are amplified because the attack surface expands dramatically with each connected device. We're addressing this through distributed security approaches where TinyML security models run on the edge devices themselves rather than relying solely on centralized protection.

Interoperability remains a significant hurdle. To address this, we're working on standardization efforts that focus on semantic interoperability rather than just syntactic connectivity. This means devices will not only be able to exchange data but also understand the meaning and context of that data across different platforms and manufacturers.

Question from NI Komang Diani:

Hello Mr Windu and Mr Yi Chi Tung, I am Ni Komang Diani. I am curious about the potential applications of this smart pole technology. Can it be implemented in various settings, including rural villages? What are the prospects for its implementation in a village setting, considering that it appears to be more suited for urban areas?

Answer: That's a very thoughtful question about extending this technology beyond urban environments. Rural implementation is absolutely possible and potentially transformative, though with some adaptations:

Fixed Wireless Access (FWA) technology is key here. Unlike urban deployments where fiber backhaul is readily available, smart poles in rural areas would leverage FWA to connect to the broader network. This provides high-bandwidth connectivity without requiring expensive fiber infrastructure throughout remote areas.

For functionality in rural settings, we're developing applications based on Robot Operating System (ROS and its successor ROS2). These platforms enable adaptive functionality based on local needs rather than urban use cases. For example, rather than traffic monitoring, rural smart poles might focus on:

1. Agricultural monitoring (soil moisture, crop health)
2. Early wildfire detection in forested areas
3. Weather monitoring for farming communities
4. Emergency communications in disaster-prone regions
5. Educational connectivity for remote schools

The modularity of our design allows customization based on local needs, and the solar charging capabilities make these poles particularly suitable for areas with unreliable power infrastructure.
